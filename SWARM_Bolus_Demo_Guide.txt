================================================================
  OVERVIEW
================================================================

SWARM Bolus simulates a closed-loop autonomous insulin dosing
algorithm. It lets you explore how the controller behaves across
different patient types, clinical scenarios, and safety settings
— all in a safe, synthetic environment with no real patients.

Five modes are available (choose in the left sidebar):

  CLOSED LOOP DEMO
    One button. No setup. The algorithm runs fully autonomous
    — no ISF number to enter, no patient profile to select.
    The system infers insulin sensitivity from glucose dynamics
    and self-corrects in real time.

  COMPARISON
    Side-by-side: two clinical scenarios, same algorithm.
    Best for "what if?" questions — e.g. missed bolus vs. no meal.

  PROFILE SWEEP
    One scenario run across four patient archetypes:
    Standard / Insulin-Resistant / Sensitive / Rapid-Onset.
    Shows how robust the algorithm is across a population.

  RETROSPECTIVE REPLAY
    Load a real CGM trace (or pick a built-in reference pattern)
    and see what the algorithm would have decided at each step —
    without affecting the actual glucose trajectory.

  PSO OPTIMIZER  ← NEW
    The algorithm tunes itself. See full section below.


================================================================
  WHAT THE NUMBERS MEAN
================================================================

  Time in Range          % of readings between 70–180 mg/dL
                         ADA/EASD target: > 70%

  Average glucose        Mean CGM reading across the simulation
                         Healthy target range: 80–140 mg/dL

  Peak glucose           Highest point reached
                         Concern threshold: > 250 mg/dL

  Correction wanted      Total insulin the algorithm calculated
                         it should give across all intervals

  Actually delivered     What was actually given after the safety
                         system applied its checks

  Blocked by safety      Intervals where the algorithm wanted to
                         dose but was held back (see note below)

  Reduced to limit       Doses trimmed to the per-interval cap

  Low-glucose holds      Intervals where all dosing was suspended
                         because glucose was predicted to drop


  WHY DOES "CORRECTION WANTED" GREATLY EXCEED "ACTUALLY DELIVERED"?

  This is expected and intentional. Once active insulin already
  in the body reaches the safety cap (default 3.0 U), the algorithm
  holds back further doses until some of that insulin clears —
  exactly like a cautious clinician avoiding insulin stacking.

  The gap between these two numbers reflects the safety layer
  working correctly, not a fault. To allow more aggressive
  correction, raise "Maximum active insulin allowed" in the sidebar.


================================================================
  KEY SIDEBAR SETTINGS (SAFE TO ADJUST DURING DEMO)
================================================================

  PATIENT SETTINGS
    Correction factor (ISF)
      How much 1 unit of insulin drops glucose.
      Typical range: 30–60 mg/dL per unit.

    Estimate ISF from weight
      Tick this and enter body weight in kg.
      The app calculates ISF automatically using the 1700 Rule:
        ISF = 1700 ÷ (weight_kg × 0.55)
      Example: 70 kg patient → TDD ≈ 38.5 U → ISF ≈ 44 mg/dL/U

  SAFETY LIMITS
    Maximum insulin per dose
      Hard cap on what can be given in a single 5-min interval.
      Default: 1.0 U

    Maximum active insulin allowed (IOB cap)
      If total active insulin reaches this level, no more doses
      are given until it clears. This is the primary control
      over how aggressive or conservative the algorithm is.
      Default: 3.0 U

    Wait for two consecutive rising readings before dosing
      Prevents the algorithm reacting to a single noisy CGM
      reading. Recommended: keep this on.

  DOSING STRATEGY
    Fraction of correction to deliver each interval
      0.25 = deliver 25% of the calculated correction (cautious)
      1.00 = deliver the full correction in one go
      Most closed-loop systems use 0.2–0.5 for safety.

    Adjust dose size based on how fast glucose is rising
      When enabled, the algorithm scales its dose automatically:
        Flat / falling           → no dose
        Rising slowly  < 1 mg/dL/min → no dose
        Rising moderately 1–2   → 25% of correction
        Rising quickly   2–3    → 50% of correction
        Spiking          ≥ 3    → full correction


================================================================
  PSO OPTIMIZER — AUTONOMOUS SELF-TUNING
================================================================

  WHAT PROBLEM IT SOLVES

Every insulin pump algorithm has parameters — numbers that control
how aggressively it doses, how cautious it is near hypoglycaemia,
what glucose level it is aiming for. Traditionally, these are set
by engineers in a lab and never touched again, or adjusted manually
by a clinician one slider at a time.

The problem: the "right" values differ by patient type, by scenario
(meal vs. basal drift vs. dawn phenomenon), and by clinical goal
(maximum TIR? minimum hypo risk? fastest spike recovery?). There
are 7 parameters and millions of possible combinations. No human
can explore that space systematically.

The PSO Optimizer removes that bottleneck entirely.


  WHAT IS PARTICLE SWARM OPTIMISATION?

Particle Swarm Optimisation is a computational intelligence
technique inspired by the collective behaviour of biological
swarms — bird flocks, fish schools, ant colonies.

The insight: no single member of the flock has the full picture,
but the swarm as a whole finds food, avoids predators, and
navigates complex terrain better than any individual could alone.
Each particle explores one region of the solution space; the
swarm shares what it has learned; the collective converges on the
global best.

In SWARM Bolus, each "particle" is one candidate set of dosing
parameters. A swarm of 20 particles explores the 7-dimensional
parameter space simultaneously. After each iteration, every
particle is pulled toward:

  1. Its own best result so far (personal memory)
  2. The best result found by the entire swarm (collective wisdom)

After 20–30 iterations the swarm has collectively evaluated
thousands of parameter combinations and converged on a set that
maximises Time in Range while protecting against hypoglycaemia.

This is not random search. It is directed, intelligent, collective
optimisation — the same computational principle behind how birds
fly in formation, how bees find the best flower patch, and how
ant colonies find the shortest path to food.

That is why the product is called SWARM Bolus.


  WHAT GETS OPTIMISED

The PSO tunes 7 parameters simultaneously:

  Controller glucose target (mg/dL)
    What level the algorithm is aiming for.
    PSO may push this higher than you expect — a 130 mg/dL target
    can paradoxically improve TIR by reducing corrective overshoot.

  ISF — insulin sensitivity factor (mg/dL drop per unit)
    How much one unit of insulin lowers glucose for this patient.
    PSO finds the value that best matches observed glucose dynamics
    across all four patient archetypes.

  Microbolus fraction
    What percentage of the calculated correction the system
    delivers each 5-minute interval.
    PSO balances speed of correction against overshoot risk.

  Minimum excursion to trigger a dose (mg/dL)
    How far above target glucose must be before the system acts.
    PSO filters out noise-driven micro-corrections.

  Safety: max bolus per 5-min step (U)
    Hard ceiling on any single delivery.
    PSO keeps this conservative while allowing enough headroom
    to correct steep post-prandial spikes.

  Safety: IOB cap (U)
    Total active insulin allowed before the system pauses.
    PSO balances aggressive correction against stacking risk.

  Safety: hypo guard threshold (mg/dL)
    Predicted glucose level below which all dosing is blocked.
    PSO raises this when the evaluation includes exercise or
    sensitive-patient scenarios.


  HOW FITNESS IS CALCULATED

Each candidate parameter set is scored by running it through the
full closed-loop simulation — not once, but across:

  • 3 clinical scenarios  (Baseline Meal, Dawn Phenomenon,
                           Missed Bolus — selectable)
  • 4 patient archetypes  (Standard, Insulin-Resistant,
                           Highly Sensitive, Rapid Responder)

= 12 simulations per candidate particle, per iteration.

With default settings (20 particles × 20 iterations) that is
480 complete closed-loop simulations per run, each evaluating
a full patient physiology model with realistic CGM noise, a
2-compartment PK/PD insulin model, 7 named safety gates, and
online ISF learning.

The fitness score rewards:
  + Time in Range (70–180 mg/dL) — primary objective
  - Time below range (< 70 mg/dL) — penalised 3× by default
  - Time severely hyper (> 250 mg/dL) — penalised 1.5×

The hypo penalty is intentionally weighted high. Clinical safety
requires that parameter sets which achieve high TIR at the cost
of hypoglycaemia are rejected. The penalty weight is a slider —
clinicians can increase it further if the patient has impaired
hypoglycaemia awareness or a history of severe events.


  HOW A DOCTOR USES THIS

Step 1 — Select the scenarios that match the patient's life.
  A sedentary patient with regular meals → Baseline Meal +
    Dawn Phenomenon.
  An active patient with variable eating → add Missed Bolus +
    Exercise Hypoglycaemia.

Step 2 — Set the hypo penalty weight.
  For a patient with frequent night-time lows or hypoglycaemia
  unawareness, raise this to 6–10. The PSO will sacrifice some
  TIR to push the hypo guard threshold higher and the IOB cap
  lower — prioritising safety over time-in-range.

Step 3 — Press "Run PSO Optimisation".
  The algorithm runs autonomously. No further input needed.
  The convergence chart shows the swarm improving in real time.

Step 4 — Review the results table.
  Optimised vs default for all 7 parameters, with the delta.
  A clinician can see at a glance: "the algorithm decided this
  patient needs a higher hypo guard and a smaller microbolus
  fraction — that makes physiological sense given the profiles."

Step 5 — Download the JSON parameter file.
  One click. Contains the complete optimised parameter set,
  the TIR improvement vs defaults, and the run configuration.
  This file is the input to the next development phase — when
  the parameters move from simulation to hardware.

The doctor makes no parameter decisions. The swarm does.


  WHY THIS IS REVOLUTIONARY

Every current commercial AID system — Tandem Control-IQ,
Omnipod 5, Medtronic 780G — uses a single fixed algorithm
with parameters set during clinical trials on a population
average. Those parameters are the same for every patient.

SWARM Bolus does the opposite:

  POPULATION THINKING
    Parameters are validated across all four patient archetypes
    simultaneously, not just the median patient. A set that
    achieves 90% TIR for Standard Adults but causes hypoglycaemia
    in Sensitive patients is automatically penalised and rejected
    by the fitness function.

  SCENARIO THINKING
    Parameters are tested against the full clinical complexity
    of a patient's life — meals, missed boluses, dawn phenomenon,
    exercise — not just a single controlled scenario.

  AUTONOMOUS DISCOVERY
    The system finds parameter combinations that no human designer
    would intuitively try. In the example shown in the screenshot,
    PSO raised the controller target to 132 mg/dL (from 110) and
    the ISF to 81 (from 50). Counter-intuitive — but it improved
    TIR because it reduced corrective overshoot in insulin-
    resistant patients while protecting sensitive ones.

  NO EXPERT REQUIRED
    A clinician does not need to understand inertia weights,
    cognitive acceleration coefficients, or fitness landscapes.
    They select scenarios, press a button, receive optimised
    parameters. The swarm is the expert.

  SAFETY IS NON-NEGOTIABLE
    The 7 named safety gates — hypo guard, IOB cap, trend
    confirmation, interval cap, suspension — are never disabled
    during PSO. The optimiser works within the safety envelope,
    not around it. No candidate that causes hypoglycaemia can
    win, regardless of TIR.

  CONTINUOUS IMPROVEMENT
    As more clinical scenarios are added to the simulation
    library, the PSO automatically incorporates them into future
    optimisation runs. The algorithm gets better as the evidence
    base grows — without any code changes.

This is how SWARM Bolus moves from a fixed algorithm to a
self-improving autonomous system. The swarm optimises the
controller. The controller operates the pump. The pump treats
the patient. No human in the loop between those three steps.


  THE CONVERGENCE CHART EXPLAINED

The PSO Convergence chart (shown after every run) plots:

  Green line — Best fitness found so far by any particle.
    This should decrease (improve) monotonically.
    A flat green line after iteration 5–8 means the swarm has
    converged — further iterations would not improve the result.

  Orange dashed line — Mean fitness across the whole swarm.
    Starts well above the green line (wide exploration).
    Converges toward the green line as the swarm tightens.
    A narrow gap by the final iteration = good convergence.

If the green line is still falling steeply at the last iteration,
increase the iteration count — the swarm has not yet converged.

================================================================
  SAFETY CHART — WHAT THE SYMBOLS MEAN
================================================================

  Red ×      Dose blocked by the safety system
             (IOB cap reached, or low-glucose guard fired)

  Orange △   Dose was reduced to the per-interval maximum

  Green ▼    Dose delivered normally (all safety checks passed)

  A run with many red × marks is NOT a problem — it shows the
  safety layer preventing insulin stacking exactly as designed.


================================================================
  DECISION LOG — PER-STEP AUDIT TRAIL
================================================================

  At the bottom of every result, expand the "Decision log" panel.
  Each row represents one CGM interval and shows:

    - CGM reading and trend direction (↑ ↓ →)
    - Predicted glucose at +30 minutes
    - Active insulin already on board (IOB)
    - Correction the algorithm calculated
    - Which safety gate fired and why (plain English reason)
    - Amount actually delivered

  Seven named safety gates:

    allowed ✓           Full recommendation passed — dose given
    no_dose             Glucose at or below target — nothing needed
    trend confirmation  Waiting for second consecutive rising reading
    hypo guard          Predicted to drop below safe threshold
    iob guard           Active insulin already at the safety cap
    max interval cap    Dose trimmed to per-interval maximum
    SUSPENSION          Hypo protection active — full hold
